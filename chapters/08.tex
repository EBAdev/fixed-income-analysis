\chapter{Forward rate models}
Classical short rate models do not fit the currently observed yield curve. We have solved this with time-inhomogeneous models. However, it is not (from an economic point of view) reasonable that the entire yield curve is driven by the short rate. Moreover, it is hard in a short rate model to obtain a realistic volatility structure for forward rates while retaining tractability of the model. A more natural approach for fitting the yield curve is to start from the observed yield curve and then model the dynamics of the entire curve. The Heath-Jarrow-Morton (HJM) approach models the instantaneous forward rates.

\begin{itemize}
  \item An approach to model the entire curve of forward rates.
  \item The HJM restriction pins down the drift of the forward rates.
  \item The volatility of forward rates should be bounded from above.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Forward rates}
The continuously compounded forward rate prevailing at time $t$ for the period $[T, T+\delta]$ is

$$
f_{t}^{T, T+\delta}=-\frac{\ln \left(B_{t}^{T+\delta}\right)-\ln \left(B_{t}^{T}\right)}{\delta} .
$$

The instantaneous $T$-maturity forward rate at time $t$ is

$$
f_{t}^{T}=\lim _{\delta \rightarrow 0} f_{t}^{T, T+\delta}=-\frac{\partial \ln \left(B_{t}^{T}\right)}{\partial T}=y_{t}^{T}+(T-t) \frac{\partial y_{t}^{T}}{\partial T} .
$$

(The forward rate curve $f_{t}^{T}$ consists of infinitely many rates).\\
Suppose the current forward rates $T \mapsto f_{0}^{T}$ are observed. For each $T$, we model the dynamics of $f_{t}^{T}$ as

$$
\mathrm{d} f_{t}^{T}=\alpha\left(t, T,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} t+\sum_{i=1}^{n} \beta_{i}\left(t, T,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} z_{i t}, \quad 0 \leq t \leq T
$$

where $z_{1}, \ldots, z_{n}$ are independent $\mathbb{P}$-BMs.

\begin{itemize}
  \item The current yield curve is matched since we observe $T \mapsto f_{0}^{T}$.
  \item We have 1 SDE for each $T$ all driven by the same $n$ random shocks.
  \item Drift/volatility may depend on the entire forward rate curve $\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}$ prevailing at time $t$.
  \item Hence, infinitely many state variables (PDE approach not suitable), so we use martingale pricing.
  \item Typically, drift/volatility terms have simpler forms $\left(\alpha(t, T)\right.$ or $\left.\alpha\left(t, T, f_{t}^{T}\right)\right)$.\\
$-r_{t}=f_{t}^{t}$ 
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Zero-coupon bond price}
The price $B_{t}^{T}$ under $\mathbb{P}$ of a zero-coupon bond price maturing at time $T$ satisfies

$$
\mathrm{d} B_{t}^{T}=B_{t}^{T}\left(\mu^{T}\left(t,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} t+\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sigma_{i}^{T}\left(t,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} z_{i t}\right)
$$

where

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mu^{T}\left(t,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) & =r_{t}-\int_{t}^{T} \alpha\left(t, u,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} u+\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\sigma_{i}^{T}\left(t,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right)\right)^{2} \\
\sigma_{i}^{T}\left(t,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) & =-\int_{t}^{T} \beta_{i}\left(t, u,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} u
\end{aligned}
$$

Proof. Consider the case of $n=1$. Then (suppress dependency on $\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}$ )

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{d} f_{t}^{T} & =\alpha\left(t, T,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} t+\beta\left(t, T,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} z_{t} \\
& =\alpha(t, T) \mathrm{d} t+\beta(t, T) \mathrm{d} z_{t}
\end{aligned}
$$

Denote

$$
Y_{t}=\int_{t}^{T} f_{t}^{s} \mathrm{~d} s
$$

Using Leibnitz' rule on $Y_{t}$ we have

$$
\mathrm{d} Y_{t}=\left(-r_{t}+\int_{t}^{T} \alpha(t, u) \mathrm{d} u\right) \mathrm{d} t+\left(\int_{t}^{T} \beta(t, u) \mathrm{d} u\right) \mathrm{d} z_{t}
$$

By using Itô's Lemma on

$$
B_{t}^{T}=\mathrm{e}^{-\int_{t}^{T} f_{t}^{s} \mathrm{~d} s}=\mathrm{e}^{-Y_{t}}=g\left(Y_{t}\right), \quad\left(g^{\prime}\left(Y_{t}\right)=-\mathrm{e}^{-Y_{t}}, \quad g^{\prime \prime}\left(Y_{t}\right)=\mathrm{e}^{-Y_{t}}\right)
$$

we find

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{d} B_{t}^{T} & =-\mathrm{e}^{-Y_{t}} \mathrm{~d} Y_{t}+\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{e}^{-Y_{t}}\left(\mathrm{~d} Y_{t}\right)^{2} \\
& =-\mathrm{e}^{-Y_{t}}\left(\left(-r_{t}+\int_{t}^{T} \alpha(t, u) \mathrm{d} u\right) \mathrm{d} t+\left(\int_{t}^{T} \beta(t, u) \mathrm{d} u\right) \mathrm{d} z_{t}\right)+\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{e}^{-Y_{t}}\left(\int_{t}^{T} \beta(t, u) \mathrm{d} u\right)^{2} \mathrm{~d} t \\
& =B_{t}^{T}\left(r_{t}-\int_{t}^{T} \alpha(t, u) \mathrm{d} u+\frac{1}{2}\left(\int_{t}^{T} \beta(t, u) \mathrm{d} u\right)^{2}\right) \mathrm{d} t-B_{t}^{T}\left(\int_{t}^{T} \beta(t, u) \mathrm{d} u\right) \mathrm{d} z_{t}
\end{aligned}
$$

\subsection{HJM drift restriction}
Proceeding as in the proof above, we find that the drift rate of the zero-coupon bond under $\mathbb{Q}$ is

$$
r_{t}-\int_{t}^{T} \hat{\alpha}\left(t, u,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} u+\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\int_{t}^{T} \beta_{i}\left(t, u,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} u\right)^{2}
$$

Meaning the drift must equal $r_{t}$ in the absence of arbitrage. Therefore

$$
\int_{t}^{T} \hat{\alpha}\left(t, u,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} u=\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\int_{t}^{T} \beta_{i}\left(t, u,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} u\right)^{2}
$$

Differentiating w.r.t. $T$ gives the HJM drift restriction

$$
\hat{\alpha}\left(t, T,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right)=\sum_{i=1}^{n} \beta_{i}\left(t, T,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \int_{t}^{T} \beta_{i}\left(t, u,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} u
$$

This is a beautiful result, because as $\beta_{i}$ can be estimated by data so can $\hat{\alpha}$.

\subsection{Additional stuff}
Leibnitz' rule: For any $s \in[1, T]$, let the dynamics of $\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \in[0, s]}$ be given by

$$
\mathrm{d} f_{t}^{s}=\alpha_{t}^{s} \mathrm{~d} t+\beta_{t}^{s} \mathrm{~d} z_{t}
$$

Let's define $Y_{t}=\int_{t}^{T} f_{t}^{s} \mathrm{~d} s$. Then

$$
\mathrm{d} Y_{t}=\left(\int_{t}^{T} \alpha_{t}^{s} \mathrm{~d} s-f_{t}^{t}\right) \mathrm{d} t+\left(\int_{t}^{T} \beta_{t}^{s} \mathrm{~d} s\right) \mathrm{d} z_{t}
$$

Forward rate dynamics under $\mathbb{Q}$ : Given the market price of risk $\lambda=\left(\lambda_{1}, \ldots, \lambda_{n}\right)^{T}$, Girsanov's Theorem states that $\mathrm{d} z_{i t}^{\mathbb{Q}}+\lambda_{i t} \mathrm{~d} t$ is a Wiener process under $\mathbb{Q}$, hence

$$
\mathrm{d} f_{t}^{T}=\hat{\alpha}\left(t, T,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} t+\sum_{i=1}^{n} \beta_{i}\left(t, T,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \mathrm{d} z_{i t}^{\mathrm{Q}}, \quad 0 \leq t \leq T,
$$

where

$$
\hat{\alpha}\left(t, T,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right)=\alpha\left(t, T,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right)-\sum_{i=1}^{n} \beta_{i}\left(t, T,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right) \lambda_{i t}
$$

Procedure for using HJM models: ( $\mathrm{n}=1$ for simplicity). Pick some $\beta(t, T)$ (short form of $\left.\beta\left(t, u\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right)\right)$. The drift is given by

$$
\hat{\alpha}(t, T)=\beta(t, T) \int_{t}^{T} \beta(t, u) \mathrm{d} u
$$

Observe current forward rate $T \mapsto f(0, T)$ from the market. Then, forward rates are given by

$$
f(t, T)=f(0, T)+\int_{0}^{t} \hat{\alpha}(u, T) \mathrm{d} u+\int_{0}^{T} \beta(u, T) \mathrm{d} z_{u}^{\mathrm{Q}}
$$

We can find bond prices by $B_{t}^{T}=\mathrm{e}^{-\int_{t}^{T} f_{t}^{s} \mathrm{~d} s}$. Given $B_{t}^{T}$ and $f_{t}^{T}$ we may find other derivative prices. Note, we do not need to specify drift.

The Ho-Lee model: (extended Merton). A one-factor model with $\beta\left(t, T,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right)= \beta>0$. The HJM drift restriction is

$$
\hat{\alpha}\left(t, T,\left(f_{t}^{s}\right)_{s \geq t}\right)=\beta^{2}(T-t) .
$$

$T$-forward rates

$$
f_{t}^{T}=f_{0}^{T}+\int_{0}^{t} \beta^{2}(T-u) \mathrm{d} u+\int_{0}^{t} \beta \mathrm{~d} z_{u}^{\mathbb{Q}}
$$

For $T=t$ we have

$$
r_{t}=f_{t}^{t}=f_{0}^{t}+\frac{1}{2} \beta^{2} t^{2}+\int_{0}^{t} \beta \mathrm{~d} z_{u}^{\mathrm{Q}}, \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \mathrm{~d} r_{t}=\hat{\varphi}(t) \mathrm{d} t+\beta \mathrm{d} z_{t}^{\mathrm{Q}}
$$

with $\hat{\varphi}(t)=\frac{\partial f_{0}^{t}}{\partial t}+\beta^{2} t$.\\
Market models: The entire forward rate curve is the modeling object in the HJM approach. However, instantaneous forward rates are not observed in practice. This lead\\
researchers to propose market models that rely on observed forward rates. They use periodically compounded interest rates and take the currently observed yield curve as given. It is possible to get Black-Scholes type formulas for caps, floors and swaptions. No model gives exact closed-form formulas for the prices of caps/floors and swaptions simultaneously.
